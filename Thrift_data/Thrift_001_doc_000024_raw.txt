fan of Virginia Woolf’s writings.Â  But living where I do in East Sussex, we are
surrounded by Woolf places, including only a few miles from here the village of Rodmell where
she ended her life by drowning herself in the River Ouse and where you can go and visit the
National Trust property Monks House , the Woolf’s country retreat.

However, I am interested in the Bloomsbury set as a whole and any new biography is worth a
look.Â  This new one by Elizabeth Wright is an ideal introduction to Woolf’s life, while
also providing some interesting discussion of her relationships with her
fellow-Bloomsburys.Â Â  It also acts as a useful literary history because it covers her
personal circumstances as she wrote each of her books and articles – including references
to her many breakdowns and times of “mania”.Â  And at 112 pages, its not going to take
very long to read.



Virginia was born in 1882, into a well-off, but troubled family.Â  Her mother died when
VirginiaÂ  was only 13 and her father, Leslie Stephen, was unable to comfort his children,
“making emotional demands for sympathy which ignored the claims of others to their own
sadness and pity”. Until this point, Virginia’s childhood had been idyllic, but without
her mother everything changed.Â  She had to cope with sexual molestation from her half
brothers (her father had been married before).Â  She grew into a bookish young woman and
attempts to introduce her into “Society” failed, for her social skills were not of the
flirtatious, seductive type required to court the vacuous sons of aristocrats. She was a
voracious teenage reader as shown by her diaries and reading lists (which covered biography,
history, travel-writing and natural science)Â  and she boasted of reading four books at
once.

Virginia’s father died in 1904 when Virginia was only 22 years old and Elizabeth Wright
tells us that she “lapsed into madness” and attempted suicide by throwing herself out of a
window. After a long period of convalescence in Yorkshire she returned to London and began her
career as a writer, with her first article appearing in December 1904, nine months after her
father’s death.Â  A year later, she was writing for four journals including the Times
Literary Review and The Guardian.

Before long, we read of the emergence of the Bloomsbury Group – her future husband Leonard
Woolf listed them as Vanessa, Virginia and Adrian Stephen, Clive Bell, Lytton Strachey, John
Maynard Keynes, Duncan Grant, E.M. Forster, Saxon Sydney Turner, Roger Fry, Desmond, Julian
and Quentin Bell and David Garnett.Â  These people, and those who joined later were
incredibly influential in the worlds of art, economics, politics, social action and public
culture such that to this day they are a model for innumerable groups and networks (and
providing inspiration for countless interior designers who seem fascinated by their
ambience – see this website for one of innumerable companies who invoke the Bloomsbury
spirit in their publicity).

Leonard Woolf married Virginia when she was 30, – a marriage of minds rather than
bodies.Â  but Viriginia was soon to struggle with bouts of severe depression and made
several suicide attempts.Â  Virginia records the onset of her illness in her diaries,
“Oh, its beginning, its coming – the horror – theÂ  physically like a painful wave
swelling about the heart – tossing me up”.Â  She wrote Mrs Dalloway in this period about
a shell-shocked soldier who commits suicide.

I won’t summarise the whole book, but I particularly enjoyed reading about the Woolf’s
time at Monks House in Sussex, where apart from the well-recorded servant problems (See my
review of Mrs Woolf and the Servants ), Leonard and Virginia found the peace and tranquillity
required to do the thinking and planning so necessary for a writer’s career.Â  The
furniture in the house was painted by Vanessa Bell and Duncan Grant and books and apples lay
everywhere – Leonard “loved the bountiful garden” and lived there until his death in
1969.

I hadn’t realised until reading this book that Virginia and Leonard were the founders of
Hogarth Press , the publishers of so many influential writers of the time.Â  In the early
days, Virginia helped sort the type, an activity which she found calming, “We get so
absorbed . . . we can’t stop, I see that real printing will devour one’s entire life”.

Throughout her life, Virginia was severely troubled by mental illness but struggled
heroically to contain it.Â  At one point she gave herself a “writing prescription” –
“The way to rock oneself back into writing is this.Â  First gentle exercise in the
air.Â  Second the reading of good literature”.Â  She fell in love with the work of
Marcel Proust, enthusing to Roger Fry,

Proust so titillates my own desire for expression that I can hardly set out the sentence. Oh if I
could only write like that! I cry.Â  And at the moment such is the astonishing vibration and
saturation and intensification that he procures – there’s something sexual in it –
that I feelÂ  I can write like that and seize my pen and then I can’t write like that.Â 
Scarcely anyone so stimulates the nerves of language in me: it becomes and obsession .

Virginia’s story ends of course with her lonely death.Â  The war years had removed the
audience for Virginia’s articles and books and she felt she was writing into a void, “No
audience.Â  No echo.Â  That’s part of one’s death” . . . “Its odd to feel one’s
writing into a vacuum – no-one will read it. I feel the audience has gone”. I recently
walked down the lane from Monks House and imagined what Virginia must have felt like on her
final stroll down to the river bank.Â  Its a lonely place, the river is hard to get to and you
have to struggle through under-growth to get near it.Â  No wonder it took a few days for her
body to be found.Â  A sad end indeed.

I started this article by saying that I am not a great fan of Woolf’s writings.Â  I would say
until reading this book, I had a mild antagonism to her, stimulated in part by the Woolf
band-wagon with all its faux-Bloomsbury artefacts and designs, and also by lack of
enthusiasm for her books.Â  However, this little book has made me see Woolf in the context of
her times as anÂ  influential publisher, critic and opinion-former who had a huge
influence on the literary world of the time while battling with terrible depression and
illness.

Her hundreds of articles made her a well-known name in her era, deservedly so, for while I think
of myself as a voracious reader, Virginia puts me to shame with a reading capacity which far
exceeded my own.Â  I am grateful to Elizabeth Wright for putting this book together – most
people will not need a longer biography than this for, despite its short length, it surely
contains the essence of Virginia Woolf.Â  It deserves to be widely purchased by those like
me who may have allowed prejudice against her rather precious style of writing to influence
their opinions of her.
